About me

In the lab I use ultrafast lasers to create molecular movies, study molecular dynamics, and synthesize new molecules and materials. For my thesis, I am developing a framework for creating molecular movies from Coulomb explosion imaging experiments. In the spirit of open and reproducible science, I plan to make my data, code, and complete analysis methods publicly available so that other groups may benefit from it.

I am passionate about teaching and have recently started working on Lovelace, an online educational platform for students to learn about science and computational methods in the natural sciences through problem solving. Projects

Ultrafast molecular dynamics probed by synchrotron radiation

The geometries and dynamics of small gas molecules may be studied by Coulomb explosion imaging (CEI), providing a means of directly probing the atomic structure of smaller molecules in the gas phase, a regime where no method is viable. CEI is usually performed using ultrashort pulse lasers with the ultimate goal of "blowing up" the molecule as fast as possible to minimize the disturbance to the molecule and ensure accurate imaging of the atomic structure.

As a proof of principle, we were able to use single X-ray photons from the Canadian Light Source synchrotron to study the dynamics of dissociative ionization in OCS using CEI. The use of single X-ray photons led to faster ionization and "blow up" compared to ultrashort pulse lasers, showing promise for greater temporal precision in CEI experiments. It also allowed us to identify a surprisingly rich set of ultrafast molecular dynamics for the first time.

Publications

  • Ultrafast molecular dynamics of dissociative ionization in OCS probed by soft X-ray synchrotron radiation, A. Ramadhan, B. Wales, I. Gauthier, R. Karimi, M. MacDonald, L. Zuin, J. Sanderson, Journal of Physics B: Atomic, Molecular, and Optical Physics (2016). [arXiv]

Molecular Motion Movies and Geometry Reconstruction Using Bayesian Statistics (MMMGRUBS)

For my thesis, I am developing a rigorous computational framework to create molecular movies using Coulomb explosion imaging, a technique of studying the ultrafast dynamics of small molecules. This is a highly nuanced problem that has only recently been tractable enough to tackle computationally.

Previous analyses are usually done strictly in momentum space for 3-4 atom molecules, however this method will allow for a more satisfying position space analysis that scales up to molecules with several atoms. The main problem of mapping momentum vector measurements to molecular geometries is tackled as a nonconvex optimization problem, making use of recent explosive research activity in machine learning. A Bayesian statistics approach, developed in collaboration with the Department of Statistics and Actuarial Science, is used to interpret the resulting geometries and naturally accounts for measurement uncertainty.

I plan to make our source code and complete analysis methods publicly available as well as tutorials on how the data analysis should be carried out so that other groups may use and improve on it.


Easy end-cap control in polyyne synthesis

The traditional synthesis method for polyynes, the allotrope of carbon with chemical structure (−C≡C−)n, is a challenging and dangerous multistep procedure that provides little control over the their end-caps. Polyynes are of great interest in interstellar chemistry and especially in nanotechnology as potential elements for molecular machines and carbon cluster precursors. Their end-caps may endow them with extra functionality and so a safe and controllable synthesis procedure is highly desirable.

By irradiating different liquid solvents with ultrashort laser pulses, we are able to easily synthesize long-chain polyynes and demonstrate end-cap control for methyl caps. Using high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC), we have confirmed the synthesis of polyynes up to C18H2 and methyl-capped polyynes up to HC14CH3.

Publications

  • Easy end-cap control in the synthesis of long-chain polyynes by intense ultrashort laser pulse irradiation, A. Ramadhan, M. Wesolowski, T. Wakabayashi, H. Shiromaru, T. Fujino, T. Kodama, W. Duley, J. Sanderson, Manuscript in preparation (2016).

Synthesis of graphene oxide gels and thin films with tunable properties

Graphene has attracted an enormous amount of attention over the past decade owing to its peculiar properties and vast applicability. However, large-scale single layers of pristine graphene are difficult to obtain and thus much research has focused on graphene oxide gels which may be used to produce high-quality graphene. These gels have their own applications too including drug delivery and sensor engineering. To satisfy the large demand for graphene oxide gels, an efficient production method is highly desirable.

By irradiating aqueous graphene oxide with femtosecond laser pulses, we were able to convert the solution into a gel with physical and chemical properties comparable with those of a monolayer graphene sheet. Furthermore, changing the laser pulse properties allows for the tuning and control over these physical and chemical properties. A deeper understanding of this relationship will allow for the synthesis of gels with tunable properties suitable for a wide variety of applications.

Publications

Project Lovelace: Learn science through programming and problem solving

Project Lovelace is an open online platform for learning about science and computational methods through programming and problem solving. It is a collection of computational science problems and tutorials taken from all branches of the natural, social, and mathematical sciences. Each problem teaches a scientific application (e.g. locating earthquakes, calculating Howitzer firing tables) and requires the use of scientific insight and some programming skills to solve. Tutorials teach computational methods that students and researchers may find useful (e.g. solving differential equations, Bayesian inference) and may be required knowledge for some problems.

While Project Lovelace is very new, we are aiming to cater to the scientific curiousity of everyone with a wide variety of problems from everyday scientific applications up to current research topics. Ultimately, we hope the problems and tutorials may be used in high school classes and university courses, especially to complement courses that lack a computational portion as computational methods have become ubiquitous in almost every field of science.

The name commemorates Ada Lovelace who proposed the first algorithm to be run on a computer in the 1840's.
