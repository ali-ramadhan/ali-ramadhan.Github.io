Finding earthquake epicenters

Useful to know: trigonometry, circles.

  • Description
  • Submissions
  • Notes and references
  • Forum
  • Solution

An earthquake occurs at some unknown location $(x,y), -100 < x,y < 100$ where $x,y$ are in kilometers (km). Modeling the Earth 's surface as a flat 2D plane, the earthquake emits seismic waves which travel through the Earth and are detected by three seismographs. Given the arrival times of the seismic waves, and the locations of the three seismographs, determine the earthquake's source or epicenter $(x,y)$. Assume the seismic waves travel at a constant 6 km/s. (We 're also assuming that the earthquake happens very close to the surface and that the seismographs are close to each other so the Earth's curvature is negligible.)

Just for a mathy lorem ipsum here is the Navier-Stokes equation in a non-inertial reference frame

$\displaystyle \rho \frac{D \mathbf{u}}{D t} = - \nabla \bar{p} + \mu \, \nabla^2 \mathbf u + \tfrac13 \mu \,\nabla (\nabla\cdot\mathbf{u}) + \rho\mathbf{g} - \rho \left(2\mathbf\Omega\times\mathbf u + \mathbf\Omega\times\mathbf\Omega\times\mathbf x + \frac{d\mathbf U}{dt} + \frac{d\mathbf \Omega}{dt}\times\mathbf x\right)$
where $\mathbf x$ and $\mathbf u$ are measured in the non-inertial frame. The first term in the parenthesis represents Coriolis acceleration, the second term is due to centripetal force, the third is due to the linear acceleration of $K'$ with respect to $K$ and the fourth term is due to the angular acceleration of $K'$ with respect to $K$.


Input: The $x$ and $y$-coordinates of the seismograph (in kilometers) and the arrival times of the seismic waves.


Output: The $(x,y)$ coordinate of the earthquake in kilometers.


Example input

8.382 -58.003 12.860 -13.590 73.976 22.847 77.291 7.508 5.767

Example output

79.0674 -27.0524
